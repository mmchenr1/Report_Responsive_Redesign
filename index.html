Responsive Redesign of Suzanne Collins' Website

For this assignment, I chose a page of a website that exhibits poor design and redisigned it to be more usable and accessibile. The process consisted of analyzing my chosen page, speed sketching design options, creating lo-fi wireframes, a style guide, hi-fi wireframes, and finally putting it all into code. An important part of this redesign process is the responsiveness of the site I designed to adapt to different dimensions and layouts. During the design process, I specifically considered mobile, tablet, and desktop layouts and used responsive units when needed.

Choosing a Page to Redesign

I chose to redesign the "Works" page of the author Suzanne Collins' site . Currently, the page layout does not lend itself to straightforward and obvious navigation. More specifically, here are a list of issues with the original site:

  • To access more information about a particular book, users can click on the title of the book in the middle section of the page, but this text isn't indicated to be a link and rather looks like just a header, resulting in questionable learnability.
  • It's not obvious what the user should be doing from the page design, causing issues with learnability and memorability. Assuming that the purpose of the site is to sell Collins' books, the design doesn’t lend itself to letting the user navigate to a purchasing point.
  • The book descriptions in the middle section of the page lack a consistent rule for ordering, with some series listed backwards chronologically and others forwards.
  • The page has responsiveness issues! At the tablet-sized dimensions, there are obvious mistakes in the styling, like an empty yellow box appearing in the upper right corner where a menu button will appear once in mobile dimensions.
  • The layout of the page on mobile doesn't seem to be designed with user experience in mind, but rather is a simple transfer of components from a horiztonal to vertical flexbox . The left panel is moved under the main panel, resulting in a long section of book covers, all of different dimensions and alignments.
There are also considerable accessibility concerns with the original design. The color scheme of yellow on white creates many low contrast components, epecially in the navigation bar where the font weight is already very low, adding to readability issues. There are also multiple instances of empty header elements that seem to have been used to add spacing between elements.

Annotations:
  1. Books are organized according to series, increasing efficiency and memorability for frequent users.
  2. There is a purchase button under each book, so that users have easy access to the books being advertised.
  3. All information about a book is located on the main page! To access different book information, there are buttons on the series carousel that clearly mark scrolling left or right. The number of books in the series is marked in the navigation panel along with a user's position in the carousel. Additionally, upcoming books are visible.
  4. By rearranging the layout for different screen sizes, the responsiveness issues with the first page are corrected.
  5. On mobile specifically, a series dropdown is introduced to help users quickly navigate to the series of their choice, rather than having to scroll through long sections.
  6. On tablet and mobile, the information from the quick links footer is moved to another page that is accessible through the menu button on the upper left corner of the screen. Because the screen width is smaller, scrolling through the series sections will take longer than on desktop, so putting the quick links in a place that is accessible from the top of the page allows for easier access.

High Fidelity Mockup

Over the course of creating the high fidelity mockups seen below, the colors changed often to ensure that elements on the page have a high contrast ratio. Additionally, on the mobile design, I added a dropdown menu to easily jump to a series section on the page because there is considerably more scrolling involved when moving the book descriptions to a vertical format.

Coding the Redesigned Page

Finally, it was time to code it all up. Using pure HTML and CSS, I translated my Figma mockup to a webpage. This process was very smooth and only required some resizing of font (on Figma the font seemed smaller than it rendered in HTML) and slight adjustments to colors for contrast. The finished product is deployed HERE. The buttons and book carousels are mocked and conform to the style guide in their static state. If I had more time, I would have liked to add the tablet and mobile menu screen so that the navbar functionality is still viewable from this page in all screen formats (it's designed for, but not implemented because of the mocked menu button).

Takeaways

This project taught me the importance of good wireframes! A good low fidelity frame lends to a better process of creating a high fidelity mockup, which then translates to a painless coding process. I learned that the more planning that happens earlier in the development process, the smoother it is overall. I also learned to check my color contrasts! It wasn't until I was translating my hi-fi mockups to HTML code that I realized that some of my contrasts were not high enough.
